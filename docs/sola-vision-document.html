Chapter 1

The information gap

The travel industry has a data problem. Not a shortage of data — a bias in it.

Every major travel platform — TripAdvisor, Google Maps, Lonely Planet, Booking.com — was built on the same foundational assumption: that a traveler is a traveler. That the same hotel review, the same walking route, the same neighbourhood recommendation serves everyone equally.

It doesn't. A street that is "vibrant and lively" at midnight for one traveler can be unsafe for another. A hostel rated 4.8 stars might have no female-only dorms. A city described as "easy to get around" may have no reliable transport after dark. The information exists, but it was never collected through the lens of who is actually using it.

This is not a niche complaint. Women represent the majority of solo travelers globally. Yet the information infrastructure they depend on was designed without them as the primary user.

The gender data gap is not about missing women from datasets. It's about building entire systems — from data collection to interface design — around a default user who was never explicitly chosen, just assumed.

Adapted from Caroline Criado Perez, Invisible Women

The result is a fragmented experience. Women planning solo trips today piece together safety information from Reddit threads, closed Facebook groups, personal blogs, and word of mouth. They cross-reference multiple sources to answer questions that should be answerable in one place: Is it safe to walk alone here? What should I wear? Which neighbourhoods should I avoid at night? Where do other women actually stay?

The data to answer these questions exists — scattered across millions of individual experiences. But no platform has structured it, verified it, and made it accessible. That is the gap Sola exists to close.

What we mean by "the gap"

It's not that safety information doesn't exist. It's that no platform has ever made it the organising principle of their information architecture. Safety is always a footnote, a secondary filter, a government advisory buried three clicks deep. For women, it's the first question — and existing platforms answer it last, if at all.

Chapter 2

The default-male lens

Information platforms are not neutral. They encode the perspective of whoever designed them.

When a platform asks "What was your experience?" without asking "Who are you?", it treats all experiences as interchangeable. The review of a 25-year-old woman traveling alone in Marrakech is weighted the same as a 45-year-old man on a business trip. Statistically, the man's review is more likely to exist — men have historically been overrepresented in travel review platforms — and so the platform's aggregate picture skews toward his reality.

This is not a conspiracy. It's a design default. Most travel platforms were built by teams solving their own problems. The taxonomy of categories (hotels, restaurants, attractions), the sorting algorithms (price, rating, distance), the information hierarchy (photos first, logistics second, safety never) — all of it reflects assumptions about what travelers need. Those assumptions were not tested against the experience of women traveling alone.

Where the lens shows up

1

Data collection

Review forms don't capture the context that matters to women. "Was this area safe at night?" is not a standard question on any major platform. "Were there other solo women here?" is never asked.

2

Information architecture

Safety is not a first-class category on any major travel platform. It's not filterable. It's not structured. It lives in the free-text body of reviews, if it appears at all.

3

Recommendation systems

Algorithms optimise for popularity, not suitability. A restaurant can be the highest-rated in a city and still be a poor recommendation for a woman eating alone — if the neighbourhood is poorly lit, the staff are unwelcoming to solo diners, or there's no safe transport home after dark.

4

Content tone and framing

"Off the beaten path" is aspirational for some travelers and a risk assessment for others. Travel content rarely acknowledges this distinction. The framing assumes a universal appetite for adventure without accounting for the cost of that adventure being asymmetrically distributed.

Sola's thesis: the solution is not to add a "women's filter" to existing platforms. The solution is to build from scratch — with women as the default user, not the edge case.

Chapter 3

What Sola is

A product, a dataset, and a methodology — designed to close the information gap.

Sola is a mobile application for women who travel solo. On the surface, it combines destination guides, trip planning, safety tools, and a women-only community. But beneath the product is a more fundamental project: building a structured, verified, women-first travel intelligence layer that does not currently exist anywhere.

Every decision in Sola — from which data we collect, to how we organise it, to how it reaches the user — is made through the lens of our primary audience: women traveling alone. This is not a feature. It is the architecture.

Three layers

1. The product

A mobile app with destination guides, trip tools, safety features, and community — the interface through which users experience Sola.

2. The dataset

A structured, growing body of women-first travel intelligence — built from automated collection, editorial curation, and community contribution.

3. The methodology

A repeatable system for collecting, verifying, structuring, and surfacing information that existing platforms do not capture.

The product philosophy

Sola is product-led. We don't start with content and ask how to distribute it. We start with the questions women actually ask and build the information architecture to answer them.

This means our product decisions are not conventional. We don't sort by rating. We don't default to "most popular." We structure every destination around the decision framework that solo women actually use:

  • Is it safe for me? — Solo-friendliness signals, transport safety, cultural context, neighbourhood walkscore.
  • Can I afford it? — Structured budget breakdowns, not paragraphs. Daily cost grids for accommodation, food, transport, activities.
  • When should I go? — Best months factoring weather, crowds, events, and seasonal safety considerations.
  • What's it actually like? — Social scene, vibe, cultural notes, dress codes — the qualitative intelligence that determines whether a destination fits.
  • What do other women say? — Community Q&A from verified women, not anonymous reviewers.
  • What do I need to know right now? — Emergency numbers, embassy lookup, health info — one tap, not three clicks.

Chapter 4

Rethinking information design

Most travel platforms organise information by geography. Sola organises it by decision.

The conventional travel platform information hierarchy is: Continent → Country → City → Attraction. It's a geographic taxonomy. It mirrors a map. It's intuitive for browsing but useless for deciding.

A woman deciding whether to visit Vietnam alone is not navigating a geographic tree. She's running a decision process: Is it safe? Can I afford it? Will I enjoy it? What do I need to prepare? The information she needs cuts across the traditional hierarchy — safety data, budget data, cultural data, experiential data — and she needs it structured for comparison and scanning, not buried in paragraphs.

How Sola structures information

1

Signals over summaries

Every country and city in Sola opens with a row of structured signals: solo-friendliness level, budget tier, vibe, best months. These are scannable data points, not prose. A user can assess a destination in three seconds before deciding to read further.

2

Structured blocks over long-form

Budget information is a 2x2 grid (accommodation, food, transport, activities), not a paragraph. Health and transport info lives in collapsible sections with clear labels. Cultural notes are actionable ("dress modestly in temples") not descriptive ("the culture is traditional"). Every piece of content is formatted for the way people actually consume information on mobile: scanning, not reading.

3

Layered depth

Information is revealed progressively. The top layer is signals. The second layer is structured blocks. The third layer is community Q&A and editorial detail. A user can stop at any layer and have enough to make a decision — or go deeper if she wants to.

4

Safety as primary axis

On every other platform, safety is a sub-category of "practical information." In Sola, safety is the first thing you see. Solo-friendliness level appears before the country description. Emergency numbers are one tap from the trip dashboard. Know-before-you-go content leads with transport safety and health, not visa requirements.

The design principle

Structure creates trust. When information is clearly organised, scannable, and honest, users trust it more — and use it more. We treat information design as a product feature, not a cosmetic layer.

Chapter 5

Technology-first intelligence

Sola is a technology company that happens to operate in travel — not a travel company that uses technology.

Building the dataset we need requires more than editorial effort. The scale of information — every city, every neighbourhood, every transport route, every accommodation option, every seasonal variation — cannot be assembled manually. And even if it could, it would be outdated before publication.

Sola's approach combines automated intelligence gathering with human verification and community co-creation. The technology stack is not incidental to the product. It is the product.

Automated collection

Web scraping and API integrations gather raw data on destinations, accommodations, transport, events, safety advisories, and local infrastructure. This runs continuously — not on a publication cycle.

AI processing

Machine learning models process raw data to extract structured signals: safety indicators, budget estimates, vibe classification, seasonal patterns. AI transforms unstructured web content into the structured data points that power our guides.

Filtering and curation

Automated systems filter for relevance to solo women travelers. A nightclub review is processed differently than a hotel review. A transport safety note is weighted differently than a restaurant recommendation. The filtering logic encodes our women-first perspective.

Recommendation engine

Personalisation driven by user interests, travel history, and behavioural signals. The system learns what matters to each user and adjusts destination recommendations, content ordering, and community suggestions accordingly.

Automation as editorial voice

Our use of AI and automation is not about replacing editorial judgement. It's about scaling it. Every automated process in Sola encodes a specific editorial perspective: what does a woman traveling alone need to know about this place? The technology asks the question at scale. Humans and community verify the answers.

Chapter 6

Building the dataset that doesn't exist

The most valuable thing Sola is building is not the app. It's the data inside it.

There is no comprehensive, structured dataset of women's solo travel experiences anywhere in the world. Google doesn't have it. TripAdvisor doesn't have it. Government tourism boards don't have it. The information exists — in millions of blog posts, social media comments, WhatsApp groups, Reddit threads, and personal conversations — but it has never been collected, structured, and made queryable.

Sola is building this dataset from three sources, each reinforcing the others.

1

Automated intelligence

Web scraping, API integrations, and AI processing generate the baseline dataset. This provides coverage — we can have structured data on 40+ countries and hundreds of cities from day one, without waiting for a critical mass of users. The automation layer gives us a starting position that most community-dependent platforms take years to reach.

2

Community co-creation

Every question asked in the community, every upvote, every reply, every connection request is a data point. When a woman asks "Is it safe to walk alone at night in Chiang Mai?" and three women answer with specific details — that becomes structured intelligence about Chiang Mai's nighttime safety from the perspective of solo women. This data does not exist anywhere else because no other platform has created the conditions to generate it.

3

Behavioural signals

How users interact with content tells us what matters. Which cities do women save but not visit? Which safety sections do they read most carefully? Where do they zoom in on budget data? These signals refine our understanding of what information is most valuable and allow us to prioritise accordingly.

The verification layer

Data without trust is noise. Sola invests heavily in verification at every level of the system.

Identity verification

Users verify their identity through a selfie with a live pose prompt. This is not optional friction — it's the foundation of community trust. When a woman reads a safety recommendation, she needs to know it came from a real person.

Content verification

Automated data is cross-referenced across sources. Community-generated content is validated by engagement patterns and moderation. The system learns which contributors produce reliable information and weights their input accordingly.

The compounding effect

Each user who joins Sola makes the dataset more valuable. Each question asked, each trip logged, each place saved adds a data point that improves recommendations for every subsequent user. This is a network effect on information quality — not just user count.

Chapter 7

Women-first is the architecture, not a filter

Every feature decision in Sola is made through the same question: what does this look like for a woman traveling alone?

"Women-first" is not a marketing position. It is a product methodology. It determines what data we collect, how we structure it, what features we build, and what we deliberately exclude. Below are examples of how this methodology manifests in specific product decisions.

1

Emergency numbers on the trip dashboard, not in settings

On most travel apps, emergency information is buried in a help section or external link. In Sola, country-specific emergency numbers are on the trip Plan tab — the same screen where users check their saved places. The design decision: safety information should never require navigation to find.

2

Share-my-trip as a core feature

One tap sends a pre-written message with destination and dates to a trusted contact. This exists because sharing your itinerary with someone at home is standard practice for women traveling alone. No other travel app treats this as a primary action.

3

Mutual-consent connections

Users cannot message each other without a mutual connection request. This is not a social media paradigm — it's a safety paradigm. Women-only platforms must make unwanted contact structurally impossible, not just reportable after the fact.

4

Cultural context as practical information

Dress codes, social norms, and local customs are not "cultural enrichment" — they are safety-relevant information. Sola structures them as actionable guidance ("cover shoulders in temples," "avoid eye contact in certain areas") rather than ethnographic description.

5

Social scene as a category

Most platforms categorise nightlife as "things to do." Sola separates social scene into its own section because the decision to go to a bar alone as a woman is fundamentally different from the decision to visit a museum. The social scene section surfaces venues where solo women are welcome — not just venues that are popular.

6

Identity verification as trust infrastructure

Verification is not a premium feature or a moderation tool. It's the foundation of community trust. In a women-only space, every user needs confidence that other users are who they claim to be. The verified badge is not social proof — it's a safety mechanism.

Chapter 8

What Sola is becoming

V1 is a product. The long-term vision is an intelligence platform.

The app launching today is a consumer product: destination guides, trip planning, community, safety tools. It serves a clear user need and delivers immediate value. But the app is also the mechanism through which Sola builds something larger.

Every interaction inside Sola generates structured data about women's solo travel experiences. Every question asked, every trip planned, every destination browsed, every safety section read adds to a growing body of intelligence that exists nowhere else. Over time, this creates a compounding asset: the most comprehensive dataset of women's travel experiences, behaviours, and preferences ever assembled.

The intelligence platform

As the dataset grows, Sola evolves from a product that surfaces curated content to a platform that generates intelligence. The recommendations become more precise. The safety signals become more granular. The community answers become more authoritative. Eventually, Sola becomes the default source of truth for the question: What is it like to travel here as a woman?

What we aim to prove

Sola is a demonstration of what happens when you build an information product for a specific audience instead of for everyone. When you make that audience's needs the organising principle — not an afterthought, not a filter, not a separate section — you produce something fundamentally different. Better structured. More trusted. More useful.

The thesis is simple: the gender data gap in travel is not a social issue to be solved with awareness campaigns. It is a product opportunity. The first company to build a women-first travel intelligence layer — combining technology, community, and editorial rigour — will own a dataset and a relationship with an audience that no competitor can replicate by bolting on a filter.

Sola is not an app with a women-first perspective.

Sola is a women-first intelligence company whose first product is an app.
